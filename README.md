## Security and Insurance

Security is a top concern for crypto investors, and rightfully so. Coinbase is one of the most secure and regulated exchanges in the world. Here is what protects your funds:

### FDIC Insurance on USD Balances

Any US dollars held in your Coinbase account are stored in FDIC-insured bank accounts. This means:

- Your USD balance is insured up to $250,000 per account holder
- If Coinbase's banking partner were to fail (not Coinbase itself), your USD is protected
- This is the same protection you have at a traditional US bank

**Important note:** FDIC insurance covers USD only — not your cryptocurrency holdings. This is standard across all US exchanges.

### Cold Storage for Crypto

Coinbase stores the vast majority of customer cryptocurrency (approximately 98%) in cold storage. Cold storage means the private keys are held on hardware devices that are physically disconnected from the internet. This makes them essentially impossible to hack remotely.

The remaining ~2% is held in hot wallets (internet-connected) to facilitate daily withdrawals and trades. This hot wallet portion is covered by Coinbase's commercial crime insurance policy.

### SOC 2 Type II Certified

Coinbase has completed SOC 2 Type II audits, which are independent third-party security audits that verify:

- Access controls are properly managed
- Encryption is correctly implemented
- Monitoring and incident response systems are in place
- Data integrity is maintained

This certification is standard for enterprise software companies and financial institutions. It gives institutional and individual investors confidence that Coinbase's security practices meet a rigorous standard.

### Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

When you set up your account, Coinbase will prompt you to enable two-factor authentication. Do not skip this step. Use an authenticator app like Google Authenticator or Authy rather than SMS if possible — authenticator apps are more secure because they are not vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks.

### Security Tips

- Use a unique password you do not use anywhere else
- Do not share your account details with anyone

### Coinbase Wallet (The Separate App)

Coinbase Wallet is a completely separate app (available on iOS and Android). It is a self-custody wallet, which means:

- **Non-custodial:** You hold your own private keys. Coinbase cannot access your funds.

- **DeFi access:** Coinbase Wallet lets you connect to decentralized applications (DeFi protocols, NFT marketplaces, etc.) directly.
- **No KYC required:** Because it is self-custody, you do not need to verify your identity.
- **Best for:** Advanced users who want full control and access to DeFi.

## Tips for First-Time Buyers

Getting started with crypto is exciting, but there are some habits that separate successful long-term investors from people who have bad experiences. Here are the most important ones:

### 1. Use Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Dollar-cost averaging means buying a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals — for example, $50 of Bitcoin every week — instead of trying to time the market. This strategy:

- Removes the stress of trying to pick the perfect entry point
- Averages out your purchase price over time
- Makes investing a habit rather than a gamble

Coinbase has a **recurring buy** feature that automates this for you. Set it up once and it runs automatically.

### 2. Only Invest What You Can Afford to Lose

Crypto is volatile. Bitcoin has dropped 50–80% from its all-time high multiple times in its history, and it has also recovered and set new all-time highs each time. But not every cryptocurrency recovers.

A good rule of thumb for beginners: only invest money you would be comfortable not seeing for 3–5 years. If the money is needed for rent, an emergency fund, or a near-term expense — do not invest it in crypto.

### 3. Do Not Panic Sell

The single most common mistake new crypto investors make is selling during a dip out of fear. Crypto markets are volatile by nature — double-digit percentage drops are normal, even in healthy bull markets.

If you believe in the long-term value of what you bought, market dips are an opportunity to buy more, not a reason to sell. Of course, this only applies if you have done your research and are comfortable with the asset you hold.

### 4. Secure Your Account Before You Deposit

Before you add any money:

- Enable two-factor authentication
- Write down your 2FA backup codes and store them safely
- Double-check the email address on your account is one only you have access to

### 5. Start With the Major Coins

Bitcoin (BTC) and Ethereum (ETH) are the two most established cryptocurrencies by market cap, liquidity, and institutional adoption. For a first investment, starting with one of these is lower risk than buying a small-cap altcoin that might go to zero.

Once you understand the basics and have done more research, you can explore other assets — but there is no shame in keeping it simple.

### 6. Keep Records for Taxes

In the United States, cryptocurrency is treated as property by the IRS. This means every time you sell, trade, or spend crypto, it is a taxable event. Coinbase provides tax forms (1099s) for users who meet the reporting threshold, and you can download your full transaction history from your account.

Keep a record of every purchase and sale. Consider using a crypto tax tool like CoinTracker, Koinly, or TaxBit, which integrate directly with Coinbase.

### 7. Do Not Share Your Account Details

Legitimate crypto support will never ask for your password, 2FA codes, or private keys. If anyone contacts you claiming to be from Coinbase and asks for this information, it is a scam. Coinbase support is only available through coinbase.com — not through Twitter DMs, Discord, Telegram, or phone calls you did not initiate.
